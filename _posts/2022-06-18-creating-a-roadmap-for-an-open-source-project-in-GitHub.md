We often assume that roadmaps mean structure. We also assume that open-source means chaos. That's why this post will be about giving the beautiful chaos a bit of structure, that will allow open-source contributors to work better together.

One thing that I noticed is that open-source maintainers are overwhelmed when they hear the word **roadmap**. In most cases they assume that that means hierarchy or structure, and that's not why they do open-source. Open source in most cases is quite the opposite of your 9-5, so why do you even need a roadmap?

Roadmaps sound scary. People usually think a roadmap needs to be a detailed overview of what they may want to achieve in the next year or more. In reality, and especially in the bitcoin space which moves rapidly, a good roadmap is flexible, iterated constantly. In open-source projects, roadmaps should not be a strict set of guidance, but a tool that helps contributors work better together and provide transparency to the community.

In my experience, to create a roadmap in open-source, you need to start with big goals in mind. The best way to think of these is to set up a group call with contributors and brainstorm what are some of the important things you as a group would like to achieve in the next 12 months.

---

## Defining goals 🎯

Depending on the stage in which your open-source project is, you probably have a few goals in mind. Or through [gathered feedback](https://github.com/readme/featured/oss-decision-making)

- What do we want to achieve?
- How do we intend to achieve it?
- What are obstacles to achieving those goals?
- What can we do to overcome the obstacles?
- What are some interesting things you'd like to work on?

Start taking taking notes and answers of these questions. You can use a Google Doc, or anything else to structure it into a list. Here's a list I used when we began creating a list of goals for the Stratum V2 project.

## Breaking goals into tasks 📝

Once you define the major goals, try to think of smaller tasks that would allow you to achieve those goals. These can be discussed in follow up calls or async. To better keep track of tasks, it’s good practice to open a GitHub issue for every task. You can think of a GitHub issue as a ticket which allows you to keep a track of a task. Sometimes an issue can be a bug report that you need to address or a feature request you’d like to work on. A good Github issue provides a well-defined task, outlines the potential solution and contains a proper label. In case of a bug report it should contain detailed information about the environment, steps to replicate and log output.
